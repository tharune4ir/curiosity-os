# Listening Lab: Paraphrase, Clarify, Confirm

## The Run Mode

### 1. The Setup
Put students in triads:
- **Speaker**
- **Listener**
- **Observer**

Give each triad a short dialogue or friction card. Example:
- “I did most of the group project and no one noticed.”
- “You always interrupt me when I’m explaining.”
- “I wasn’t trying to ignore you — I just didn’t understand what you meant.”

The observer gets a tiny checklist:
- Did the listener interrupt?
- Did the listener paraphrase accurately?
- Did the listener ask a real clarifying question?
- Did the speaker confirm they felt understood?

### 2. The Activity

#### Round 1 — Paraphrase before reply
The speaker talks for 30–45 seconds.

The listener may **not** reply with opinion, advice, or defense yet.

They must first say:
- “What I’m hearing is…”
- “So you’re saying…”
- “You seem to mean…”

The speaker then answers:
- “Yes, that’s right”
or
- “Not exactly — what I mean is…”

#### Round 2 — Clarify the missing piece
Now the listener asks **one clarifying question**:
- “What part of that bothered you most?”
- “What did you expect instead?”
- “Was it the words, the tone, or the timing?”

Only after the answer can the listener respond.

#### Round 3 — Observer feedback
The observer names:
- where meaning got distorted
- whether the paraphrase matched the meaning
- whether the clarifying question improved understanding
- whether the listener slipped into “fixing mode” too quickly

Then roles rotate.

### 3. The Decompile

Focus on **accurate listening as a real skill**:

- Many conflicts grow because people reply to their assumption, not to what was actually meant.
- Paraphrasing slows down distortion.
- Clarifying questions reduce false certainty.
- Accurate listening does not mean agreement. It means reality-matching before response.
- Trust grows when people feel understood correctly, even when disagreement remains.

Close with one transfer question:

**Where in school life would this one habit prevent the most unnecessary conflict?**
