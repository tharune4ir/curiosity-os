## The Invisible Reality
Astronauts live and work in zero gravity, which is like floating in space or underwater. They must know physics to move in a spaceship (for example, pushing off walls sends them gliding across the room). Outside, they see Earth as a thin blue curve. Every action in space has an equal and opposite reaction: firing a thruster pushes the spaceship forward, like a balloon releasing air in space.

## The Mind Blowing Hook
Right now, astronauts on the International Space Station orbit Earth 16 times a day, each orbit taking only 90 minutes. They grow plants in space, weld metal on a satellite while floating, and even play sports (like soccer) in microgravity. Space travel uses rocketry and physics that scientists are still understanding.

## The Players Life
You train on Earth in simulations that mimic weightlessness (like underwater or parabolic flights). You test equipment and learn many skills (engineering, science experiments). In space, your daily tasks include maintaining the station, running scientific experiments, and doing spacewalks while tethered to the ship. Every day is scheduled to the minute because resources are limited.

## The Skill Tree
Fitness to withstand launch forces, engineering knowledge (for spacecraft systems), and adaptability to novel environments.
