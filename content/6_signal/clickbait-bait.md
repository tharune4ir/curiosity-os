## The Invisible Code
Websites often use headlines that promise something exciting, shocking or unbelievably funny. This is like fishing bait on a hook—you see the shiny lure and can’t help but click. Often, the story isn't as amazing as it seems. It’s a trick to get you to click and stay on the site, which helps the site make money from ads.

## The Digital Trap
You see a headline on social media: 'You Won’t Believe What This Celebrity Did at School!' Curious, you click. The page loads slowly and when it does, it's just a picture of a teen with a hat, and the story is boring. You feel tricked. Meanwhile, your phone had to load some hidden ads and maybe even a suspicious link that started tracking you.

## The Shield Protocol
Before you click on a headline that sounds too wild, pause and ask: 'Is this real or just a trick to get me to click?' Instead of clicking, try searching the main keywords of the headline on Google News or a reliable site to see if real sources cover it. If only weird sites have it, it's probably fake clickbait.

## The Lifeline
It’s normal to feel a bit silly for being tricked by a clickbait title. But remember, lots of people fall for this every day – even adults. There's no shame in that, and it's a learning moment. If you find something that was clickbait, you can share it with your parents or friends and say, 'Hey, I fell for this! Let’s see what we can learn from it.' Laugh about it and move on.

## The Empowerment Move
Whenever you see a headline that sounds shocking or unbelievable, wait 10 seconds before clicking. Then use Google (or another search) to check if any well-known news website has the same story. If not, just ignore it and move on. You can also report the post or hide it if the site lets you.
