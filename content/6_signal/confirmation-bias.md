## The Invisible Code
Your brain likes finding evidence for what it already believes. The algorithm knows this and shows you posts that match your likes and dislikes. It's like a friend who only agrees with you and never challenges your view. You end up believing more strongly in what you already thought, because your feed mostly proves you right.

## The Digital Trap
Let's say you believe eating vegetables is boring. You start clicking on jokes that make fun of vegetables. The algorithm notices, so it shows you more jokes about vegetables and fewer posts about how tasty they can be. Soon, almost everything on your feed says vegetables are yucky. You get more and more sure you were right, not realizing the app only let you see one side.

## The Shield Protocol
Be aware that when you see posts that all agree with you, it might be tricking your mind. Try to pause and ask: 'Would other people disagree?' Make a little habit: look for one thing that challenges your thought. It's like eating a sweet candy and then also having a sour candy to balance. If you think vegetables are boring, maybe read one healthy recipe or ask a friend who likes them.

## The Lifeline
It can feel funny or surprising when something you believed turns out to have another side. Remember: everyone has confirmation bias. Parents, teachers, even experts in school make that mistake. Talking about it with someone can help you see other sides. Saying 'I only saw one point of view and now I found another' is okay, and you'll learn.

## The Empowerment Move
When you're sure about something, do a quick fact-check: Google one sentence against what you believe and see if something different pops up. Or talk about the belief with a parent or friend and ask if they know another view. Make it a game to find one fact that doesn't fit, like a detective.
