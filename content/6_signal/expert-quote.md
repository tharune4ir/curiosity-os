## The Invisible Code
When something online confuses you, asking someone knowledgeable is smart. It's like asking a coach about a game rule you don't understand. Teachers, parents, or librarians can explain if information seems fishy, because they know more about how things work.

## The Digital Trap
You read a complicated claim about science on social media. It sounds unbelievable, but you're not sure. You consider sharing, but instead you ask your science teacher. She explains it's false and gives you real facts. You avoid spreading a lie.

## The Shield Protocol
Find a trusted adult (teacher, parent, local news app) to confirm information. If a message or video makes you unsure, tell them 'I found this online, what do you think?' They'll break it down for you. Experts know stuff only after learning it carefully, unlike internet posts that anyone can write.

## The Lifeline
Relying on a trusted adult to confirm saves you from mistakes. They will respect that you asked and will give you correct info. It also strengthens your relationship because they know they can guide you on tricky questions.

## The Empowerment Move
Write down your teacher's or librarian's phone number or an email (or even a safe group chat like family in DMs). When you see something confusing, send them a quick message asking, 'Is this real?' Instantly get help from someone you trust.
