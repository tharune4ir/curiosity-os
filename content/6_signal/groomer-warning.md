## The Invisible Code
Some bad people (predators) pretend to be kids to gain trust. They give compliments, gifts, or always agree with you at first. It's like a wolf in sheep's clothing: they try to seem like a buddy or helpful stranger to slowly trick you. Once you trust them, they might ask for secrets or personal meetings.

## The Digital Trap
You meet someone in a gaming chat who is very nice and says they are your age. They start asking personal questions ('Where do you live?', 'Are your parents around?') and give you gifts like game currency. Later they hint at meeting in person. You feel excited and nervous but something feels off, like they’re not who they seem.

## The Shield Protocol
Watch out for people online who move too fast or ask for private info. A real friend wouldn’t insist you hide things from your parents. If someone seems too perfect or always agrees with you, it could be a trick. Always talk to a parent if someone online asks odd questions or wants to meet. Trust your gut: if something feels icky, stop talking.

## The Lifeline
If you realize someone was a predator, shout it out (figuratively): tell a parent or call child helpline right away. Predators want you to keep quiet, but speaking up removes their power. Your family will never think less of you for this. They can handle it with police or experts. You did right by telling someone.

## The Empowerment Move
Make a list of at least 3 adults you can trust (parents, teachers, counselor). Put it on your phone's note app. Then tell someone in real life, 'I feel uncomfortable with someone I met online' whenever you need to. Having a reminder of helpers makes it easier to speak up.
