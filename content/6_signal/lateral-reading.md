## The Invisible Code
Lateral reading means you don't stay on one site for answers. It's like reading notes from different books in a library instead of just one. If something sounds unbelievable, you open new tabs and search about it elsewhere, to see if experts agree or disagree.

## The Digital Trap
You search a scary claim and the top link says aliens landed in India. It looks convincing with images. If you stop there, you'll believe it. But if you open a second or third tab and find all major sites say it's a hoax, you'll know not to trust that first page.

## The Shield Protocol
Habit: when you read shocking news, immediately search for the same headline on a known site or just type the headline in quotes into Google. Read a couple of different sources before believing it. It's like asking two friends for advice instead of just one.

## The Lifeline
Tell someone, 'I read something weird, I'm checking if it's true.' Engaging them makes it easier to stay calm. They might help search or discuss it. Then you make the right decision together. Breaking news sites can sensationalize, but people you trust will give you honest input.

## The Empowerment Move
Practice lateral reading right now: pick a random news headline from social media, and search it. If you find reputable sites either confirming or debunking, you just practiced fact-checking!
