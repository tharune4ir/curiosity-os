## The Invisible Code
Some groups or bots make fake accounts that all 'like' and 'share' each other's posts to trick you into thinking they're real popular content. It's like a rumor circle in school where friends all say something is cool so you think it must be true. These networks game the system to boost their numbers and influence.

## The Digital Trap
You find a page that has thousands of likes, sharing a video claiming a celebrity did something shocking. It looks real, so you trust it. But after watching, you realize it's a hoax. Those likes were just from a hidden network of fake friends. You fell for the hype and then got embarrassed when your own friends tell you the page was fake.

## The Shield Protocol
Don't trust the number of likes or shares. Check who is sharing it: if they all seem very new or have no real profile picture, it's suspicious. If a story looks fishy, search it by text on Google. And remember: real popularity often comes from varied sources, not just one small group boosting each other.

## The Lifeline
If you shared something that turned out false because you saw too many likes, it's okay. People make that mistake. The important thing is to learn, not hide. Explain to an older person, 'I shared something that turned out to be fake.' They won't blame you. Instead, they'll be proud you want to correct it.

## The Empowerment Move
When you see a post with tons of likes, scroll through the list of who liked it. If they all look the same (like bunny icons, or weird names, or they don't have any posts), it's likely a trap. Next time, screenshot suspicious posts and ask a sibling or teacher, 'Is this for real?' before sharing.
