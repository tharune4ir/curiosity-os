## The Invisible Code
Shortened URLs (like bit.ly, tinyurl) hide the real web address. It's like a wrapped candy; you see the bright wrapper but not the flavor inside. It could lead to a dangerous site instead of a legitimate one.

## The Digital Trap
A friend sends 'tinyurl.com/freegiftcode' and you click it on your phone. It asks for a login with your Google account. You think it's legit, but it was a phishing page. Now your account is compromised.

## The Shield Protocol
Before clicking short links, copy and paste them into a preview tool (or use services like 'unshorten.me') to see the real URL. If that's not easy, ask the sender who it is from. If the person is a friend, confirm with them directly that they meant to send that link.

## The Lifeline
If you clicked a shortened link and it turned out bad, tell an adult right away: 'I clicked a weird link.' They will help secure your account. Parents know all about avoiding fishy links. They'll be relieved you asked and help you fix it.

## The Empowerment Move
Next time someone posts a short link in chat, long-press it and see if your phone shows a preview of the full link. If it doesn't, screenshot it and ask a parent to check it for you.
