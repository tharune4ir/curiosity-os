## The Invisible Code
Memes and images can trick you. A photo might be old or from somewhere else. It's like seeing a photo of a boy at a playground and being told it was from your school. Trusting every picture is like believing an actor in a movie is a real life story.

## The Digital Trap
A meme shows a photo of a crowded street in Mumbai with text saying 'Lockdown is over, enjoy!'. You think lockdown rules are lifted and go out. Later you find out it's from last year and lockdown is actually still on. You feel unsafe and fooled.

## The Shield Protocol
Before believing an image, do a reverse image search (use Google Images or apps). If the photo has been online before, you'll find its real context. If not, be very skeptical. Always check the date or source of images if they support a big claim. Photos can be powerful liars.

## The Lifeline
If you acted on a meme mistake (like going out thinking rules changed), tell someone: 'I saw this meme about lockdown.' They can guide you with correct information. The goal is safety, not scolding. Clarifying real info with someone reduces your stress.

## The Empowerment Move
Try Google Lens (or image search) on a trending meme photo. Even just once, you'll see how fast it finds the real story behind it. Save a step by practicing: whenever you see an interesting image, long-press to search it.
