## The Invisible Code
When logging in, sometimes services send a One-Time Password (OTP) to your phone to verify it's really you. Scammers can ask for this OTP under false pretenses. It's like giving them the key that just arrived by mail; with it, they can get into your account immediately.

## The Digital Trap
You get a call saying 'We are from your bank, we sent you an OTP for security. Please provide it.' Trusting them, you read out the 6-digit code. Moments later, your WhatsApp has been taken over and sends messages to your friends asking for money. The scammer used that OTP to log in as you.

## The Shield Protocol
Never share your OTP with anyone. Legitimate services (bank, WhatsApp) will never ask you for it; it's only for you to type yourself. If someone calls or messages asking for it, hang up and alert a parent. Think of OTP like a bathroom key; once used, it flushes down. If you share it, they go in pretending to be you.

## The Lifeline
If you accidentally shared an OTP, tell a parent at once: 'I just gave away a secret code.' They can help you secure the account (like resetting your password). It’s an easy mistake, and telling quickly means things can be fixed before real damage happens. No adult will scold you for that.

## The Empowerment Move
Set up a password on your lock screen and accounts so even OTP alone can't log in. When using OTP, type it directly instead of copying it. If you get any strange login requests (like 'someone logged in from another device'), immediately inform an adult.
