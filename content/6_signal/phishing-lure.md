## The Invisible Code
Phishing is when someone tries to trick you into giving up personal info (like passwords) by using a fake message or link. It's like a fisherman casting a fake worm in a pond, hoping you take the bait. That email or message looks real, but it’s a trap set to steal your details.

## The Digital Trap
You get a message on WhatsApp supposedly from your teacher: 'Click this link to download your exam paper.' The link even looks like the school website. You click it and a password box pops up. You enter your school login and suddenly your account is locked. The link was fake, and now the hacker has your password.

## The Shield Protocol
Always check links before you click. If a message asks for personal info or passwords, be suspicious. Instead of clicking a link, try opening the real website yourself from the browser or ask the sender in person. Look out for tiny spelling mistakes in URLs or unknown senders. Schools and banks never ask for passwords via chat, so that's a big red flag.

## The Lifeline
If you ever click on a suspicious link or think you've been tricked, don't keep it a secret. Tell an adult right away: 'I clicked something, should I be worried?' They can help reset passwords or alert others. It's not your fault for clicking — these tricks fool even tech experts. The sooner someone helps, the better.

## The Empowerment Move
Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on all your accounts right now (like WhatsApp/Google). That way, even if someone gets your password, they still can't log in. Also, practice by checking a suspicious message: hover over or long-press a link to see where it really goes (don’t click it).
