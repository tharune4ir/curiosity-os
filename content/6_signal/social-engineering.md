## The Invisible Code
Social engineers are sneaky people who use tricks and lies to fool you into giving information or doing something unsafe. It's like a pickpocket on a train distracting you with a story while stealing your wallet. Online, they might pretend to be a friend of a friend or someone official.

## The Digital Trap
You get a message from someone saying they're your best friend's mom, needing a favor. They ask for your friend's address and birthday to send a gift. Your first instinct is to help, so you share the details. Later your friend asks why you gave out their info without asking. You realize you were fooled by someone who wasn't really their mom.

## The Shield Protocol
Always verify identities when someone requests personal info or favors. If it's unexpected, call or talk to the supposed person another way. Never feel pressured by urgency. Authentic friends and family will understand if you say 'Let me check with my parents first.' Remember: it's okay to say no or pause and confirm.

## The Lifeline
If you shared something you shouldn’t have, tell someone immediately: 'I think I was tricked online.' They will know what to do and help you correct it. Doing nothing might let the trickster get more info. You’re doing the right thing by speaking up, and adults know how to handle situations like this.

## The Empowerment Move
Practice saying 'I'll confirm with my mom/dad first' whenever someone online asks for personal information. It might feel odd, but it's a strong defense. Also, save your parents' phone numbers in your contacts so you can quickly call to check if needed.
