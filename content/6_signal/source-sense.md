## The Invisible Code
Always look at who wrote the story or post. If it’s an anonymous social media user, think twice. It's like judging a book by its cover: a book from a famous author is likely reliable, whereas one from a random person might not be trustworthy.

## The Digital Trap
You read a comment claiming a plant cures all diseases by an anonymous account. Without checking, you start believing it. Later, a doctor friend says it's false and harmful. You realize you ignored that it had no credited author or site.

## The Shield Protocol
Check the author or site name. If it's a person or organization you’ve never heard of, find their background. Reputable articles usually list author and credentials. If it's missing or bizarre (like 'HealthGuru457'), be skeptical. Trust experts, not strangers online.

## The Lifeline
Tell someone: 'I’m not sure who wrote this, is it legit?' A parent or teacher can help identify credible authors or show the real source. Discussing authorship together is a good exercise; you'll learn how to judge sources by who says them.

## The Empowerment Move
Before you read an important claim, check the webpage’s header or bottom for author or organization. Make it a habit: any time you read a 'news' post, first scroll to the top and bottom to see where it came from.
