## The Invisible Code
Before forwarding any message or meme, treat it like a detective piece of evidence. It's like a treasure map: if you share it without checking, you might be leading everyone astray. Always double-check facts or images from unknown sources.

## The Digital Trap
You get a forwarded WhatsApp saying a famous actor has died in a crash. You feel sad and share it with your friends. Later, you learn it was a hoax. You feel embarrassed you added to the false news.

## The Shield Protocol
Always ask yourself: 'Is this really true?' Instead of forwarding, do a quick search of key details. If a message doesn’t have a source or the news isn't on real sites, skip it. Think of it like checking your work in math before submitting—it prevents silly mistakes.

## The Lifeline
If you accidentally forwarded something false, tell your friend group 'Hey, I think I might have been wrong about that news.' They'll probably appreciate your honesty and might thank you for correcting it. This helps rebuild trust that you share true things.

## The Empowerment Move
Every time before forwarding, screenshot the message and search one key phrase on Google or YouTube. Spend 10 seconds doing this. It becomes a habit and keeps your feed clean.
