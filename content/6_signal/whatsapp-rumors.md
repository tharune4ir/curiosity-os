## The Invisible Code
On apps like WhatsApp, rumors and myths travel extremely fast through 'forwarded' messages. It's like a game of telephone where the message gets changed and exaggerated as it's passed around. Because these come from friends or family, you're more likely to believe them, even if they're completely false.

## The Digital Trap
Your aunt forwards a message: 'Eat a clove of garlic every morning to prevent the flu!' It sounds like a helpful tip, so you start doing it. But then you realize there's no real science behind it and your friends are making fun of your garlic breath. You realize you fell for a forwarded rumor that had no basis in truth.

## The Shield Protocol
Be very careful with 'Forwarded' messages, especially those with the 'Forwarded many times' tag. Before you believe or forward it yourself, ask: 'Is this based on truth or just a rumor?' Look up the main claim on a trusted news site. If you can't find it there, it’s likely just noise. Don't be a link in the chain of rumors!

## The Lifeline
If you receive a forwarded message that seems important or scary, talk to a parent: 'I got this message on WhatsApp, is it true?' They can help you verify it and explain why some things get forwarded without being true. They'll appreciate that you're checking before spreading the word. Together you can stop the rumor mill!

## The Empowerment Move
Whenever you see a forwarded message with a big claim, take a screenshot and ask your teacher or a parent to help you find a real source for it. If you can't find one, tell the sender: 'Hey, I checked that it might not be true.' You're being a responsible digital citizen!
