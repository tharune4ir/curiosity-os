## The Invisible Architecture
An affordance is a design property that tells you how to use something. A door handle you can grip tells you to pull. A flat plate on a door tells you to push. In digital design, a button with a shadow and rounded corners 'affords' clicking — it looks like something you can press. A link in blue underlined text affords clicking. These visual cues are called 'signifiers'. When designers remove these cues in favor of 'clean' design, users don't know what's clickable. This causes frustration and abandoned tasks.

## The Consumer Trap
Many 'modern minimalist' apps confuse users by removing all visual affordances. Flat buttons that look like plain text, swipe gestures with no visual hint, settings hidden behind unmarked icons — these feel elegant to designers but confuse new users. Apple's iPhone introduced confusing 'how do I go home?' problems when they removed the home button.

## The Builder Hack
A builder follows one rule: every interactive element on their interface must have at least one visual cue that signals its interactivity — a hover state, a shadow, an underline, a color change. Users should never have to 'discover' that something is clickable by accident.

## The Superpower
You design with empathy. You see the world through the eyes of a first-time user who has no context or assumptions. This produces intuitive products that don't need user manuals.

## The First Line Of Code
Open any website and try to identify which elements are interactive just by looking — without clicking. Then hover over random elements to see if you were wrong. Every surprise is a broken affordance — a UX lesson in real time.
