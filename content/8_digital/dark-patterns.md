## The Invisible Architecture
A dark pattern is a UI trick designed to manipulate users into doing things they didn't intend — things that benefit the company at the user's expense. Examples: the 'unsubscribe' button that's tiny gray text on a white background. The confirmation dialog where the 'confirm deletion' button is green and the 'cancel' button is red (reversed from expectation). The cookie consent screen where 'Accept All' is a big button but 'Manage Preferences' requires 5 more clicks. These are deliberate design choices to extract value from users through confusion.

## The Consumer Trap
Millions of Indians have been subscribed to services they didn't realize they were signing up for, or found charges on their cards from apps that made cancellation impossibly difficult. Every 'free trial' that requires a credit card and hides the cancel button is a dark pattern. They work on almost everyone.

## The Builder Hack
A builder with integrity uses dark patterns as a list of things NOT to do. Their philosophy: if a user needs to be tricked to take an action, that action doesn't actually serve the user — which means it won't build long-term trust or retention. Ethical design is better business long-term.

## The Superpower
You see dark patterns instantly when you encounter them. You know when you're being manipulated, and you know how to design interfaces that earn user trust instead of exploiting confusion. This distinction defines ethical builders.

## The First Line Of Code
Visit 'darkpatterns.org' (now 'deceptive.design'). Browse their real-world Hall of Shame. Recognize these patterns in apps you use daily. Once seen, they cannot be unseen.
