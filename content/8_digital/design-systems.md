## The Invisible Architecture
A design system is a library of standardized components — buttons, colors, typography, spacing rules, icons — shared across an entire product. Google's Material Design is a design system. Apple's Human Interface Guidelines is a design system. When every team building different parts of Google's products uses the same components, the entire product suite feels coherent and consistent. Without a design system, different teams create different buttons, different colors, and different interactions — producing an inconsistent, confusing product.

## The Consumer Trap
The reason Google's products (Search, Maps, Gmail, Drive) all feel like they 'belong together' despite being built by different teams — that's a design system at work. The familiarity reduces cognitive load for users. You already know how to use a new Google product before you open it, because the patterns are the same.

## The Builder Hack
A builder starting a project uses an existing open-source design system like Shadcn/UI (for React) or Material UI. In one command, they have 50 professionally designed, accessible components ready. Their startup's app looks polished from day one without hiring a designer.

## The Superpower
You can build consistent, professional-looking products rapidly by leveraging existing design systems. And when you're big enough to need your own, you understand how to build one — a skill that makes you valuable to any product team.

## The First Line Of Code
Go to 'material.io/components' — Google's Material Design component library. Click through each component. Notice how every specification — spacing, animation, color — is documented precisely. This is what a professional design system looks like.
