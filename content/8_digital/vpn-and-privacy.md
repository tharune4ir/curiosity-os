## The Invisible Architecture
A VPN (Virtual Private Network) creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and a VPN server in another location. Instead of your traffic going directly to websites, it first goes to the VPN server (encrypted), then the VPN server forwards it. Websites see the VPN server's IP, not yours. Your ISP sees only encrypted traffic going to the VPN server — they can't see which websites you visit. It's like sending all your letters to a friend in another city, who then re-sends them for you without your return address.

## The Consumer Trap
People use free VPNs and feel 'protected'. The irony: a free VPN's business model is selling your data — exactly what you're trying to protect. You solved the ISP-seeing-your-data problem by giving it directly to a random VPN company. There is no free privacy. You either pay for the service or you are the product.

## The Builder Hack
A builder knows that VPNs are used by remote teams to securely access internal company networks — not just for unblocking content. Many companies run their own private VPN servers (using WireGuard or OpenVPN) so employees can access sensitive databases from home without exposing them to the public internet.

## The Superpower
You understand what VPNs actually protect (IP address, ISP visibility) and don't protect (activity once on a website, VPN company logs). You can make informed, not marketing-driven, decisions about digital privacy.

## The First Line Of Code
Open your browser. Note your IP from 'whatismyip.com'. Enable any VPN. Note the new IP. You can see exactly what websites now see as 'you' — a different location entirely.
