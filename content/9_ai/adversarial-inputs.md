## The Black Box Revealed
Researchers discovered that AI can be fooled by tiny, precise changes to inputs that would not fool any human. Add a few invisible pixels to a picture of a school bus and a self-driving car's AI might classify it as a bird — at 99% confidence. Add a tiny sticker to a stop sign and some AI systems will misclassify it as a speed limit sign. These are called adversarial examples — inputs crafted specifically to exploit the mathematical vulnerabilities of neural networks.

## The Lazy Human Trap
People assume AI vision and AI safety systems are robust because they perform well on normal inputs. They are not. Adversarial attacks can be used to fool AI-based security systems, cheat at AI-graded exams, or bypass content filters. Robustness is not the same as accuracy.

## The Symbiosis Hack
A smart student interested in AI security studies adversarial robustness. Many companies are desperate to test their AI systems against adversarial attacks (called red-teaming). Building the skill of finding edge cases and unusual inputs that break AI is a valuable and interesting research direction.

## The Future Proof Superpower
As AI controls more of our critical systems — traffic, medical devices, fraud detection — adversarial robustness becomes a life-or-death engineering problem. The next generation of AI safety engineers will specialize in making AI systems that cannot be fooled by malicious actors.

## The Prompt Experiment
Ask AI to solve a logic puzzle. Once it gives an answer, rephrase the exact same puzzle with slightly different surface words (change names, change colors) but keep the logical structure identical. See if the AI changes its answer — this tests whether it solved the logic or recognized a surface pattern.
