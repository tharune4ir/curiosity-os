## The Black Box Revealed
Governments around the world are racing to regulate AI. The EU has the AI Act — the first comprehensive AI law, classifying AI systems by risk level and imposing strict requirements on high-risk applications (medical devices, credit scoring, police surveillance). The US uses sector-specific regulation. China requires government approval for certain AI services. India is developing its own approach. This is a new legal landscape being written right now, and the choices made in the next 5 years will shape AI deployment for decades.

## The Lazy Human Trap
Students think regulation is for politicians and lawyers. But AI regulation directly affects what AI tools they can use, what data companies can collect about them, whether an AI can reject their college application, and who is responsible when AI causes harm. The regulatory landscape shapes every AI product you use.

## The Symbiosis Hack
Follow AI regulatory developments as a student of civics and technology together. Understand the EU AI Act's risk classification. Ask: which AI systems used in your school or life are high-risk under EU law? Which are unregulated? This analytical lens makes you more informed as a citizen, a professional, and eventually a voter who will elect people who make these decisions.

## The Future Proof Superpower
AI policy is a genuinely interdisciplinary career — at the intersection of law, technology, ethics, and economics. Countries like India desperately need people who understand both the technical capabilities of AI and the policy frameworks to govern it. This is a rare combination that commands enormous professional opportunity.

## The Prompt Experiment
Ask AI: 'Explain the EU AI Act's classification of AI systems by risk level. Give me 3 examples of AI systems that would be classified as high risk, and for each, explain what requirements the company building it must meet.' Evaluate how current and accurate its regulatory knowledge is.
