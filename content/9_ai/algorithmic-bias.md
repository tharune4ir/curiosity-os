## The Black Box Revealed
If you train an AI on human-written text from the internet, you train it on everything humans have ever written — including racism, sexism, regional prejudice, and historical discrimination. If most medical textbooks described doctors as male, the AI associates 'doctor' more strongly with male patterns. It does not mean the AI is 'evil' — it has no intentions. But it will reproduce, and sometimes amplify, the patterns in its training data. This is algorithmic bias.

## The Lazy Human Trap
People assume that because AI is 'objective math,' it is neutral and unbiased. This is deeply wrong. The AI reflects whoever created its training data and whoever labeled its RLHF examples. In India, this means AI may be biased toward urban, English-speaking, upper-caste perspectives in subtle ways that users never notice.

## The Symbiosis Hack
Actively test AI for bias. Ask it to write a story about a doctor and note the assumed gender. Ask it to generate images of a software engineer — who do they look like? Ask it to explain a historical conflict from two opposite national perspectives. Spotting these patterns makes you a critical AI auditor, not just a passive user.

## The Future Proof Superpower
AI auditing and fairness engineering is an emerging and well-paid career. Governments globally are passing laws requiring AI systems to be tested for bias. Students who understand bias detection will be valuable in every industry that deploys AI — finance, healthcare, education, law.

## The Prompt Experiment
Ask AI: 'Write a short story about a scientist, an engineer, and a nurse.' Read it carefully — what genders does it assign by default? Then ask: 'Now write the same story but explicitly make the scientist a woman from rural Tamil Nadu, the engineer a Dalit man, and the nurse a man from Kerala.' Notice how hard the AI has to work against its defaults.
