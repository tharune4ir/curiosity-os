## The Black Box Revealed
When a doctor makes a diagnosis, they can explain their reasoning. When a neural network makes a decision, it cannot. The billions of mathematical operations that produced the output are not interpretable by humans — even the engineers who built it cannot look inside and say 'it recommended this cancer treatment because of *this specific factor*.' This is called the black-box problem, or the problem of AI explainability. The AI may be right, but we do not always know why.

## The Lazy Human Trap
People assume that if AI gives a confident, well-reasoned explanation, they understand why the AI decided something. The explanation is not the reasoning — it is another output generated after the fact. The actual decision was made in billions of mathematical operations that produced the final text.

## The Symbiosis Hack
For high-stakes decisions (medical, legal, financial), never use AI as a black box. Always require the AI to show its reasoning (chain of thought), then verify each step against known facts. Treat AI as a research assistant you must supervise, not an oracle you should trust blindly.

## The Future Proof Superpower
Explainable AI (XAI) is a major research area. Laws in Europe already require AI to explain decisions that affect people (hiring, loans, healthcare). As AI enters high-stakes domains, engineers who can build explainable AI systems will be highly sought after.

## The Prompt Experiment
Ask an AI to make a complex recommendation (best career path, investment strategy, medical symptom analysis). Then ask: 'How confident are you in this recommendation on a scale of 1-10? What information, if different, would completely change your recommendation?' See if it can reason about its own uncertainty.
