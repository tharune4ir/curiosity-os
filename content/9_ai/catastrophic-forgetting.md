## The Black Box Revealed
A profound difference between human and AI learning: humans can learn new skills without forgetting old ones. When you learn to ride a bike, you do not forget how to walk. But when a standard neural network is trained on new data (say, a new language), it forgets its previous training catastrophically — the new data overwrites the old patterns. This is why AI models are typically trained once on everything at once, in a massive batch, rather than learning continuously like humans.

## The Lazy Human Trap
Users expect AI to improve based on their interactions — to learn their preferences and get better over time, like a human colleague would. Most current AI systems cannot do this without catastrophic forgetting. When they are updated (retrained), they get new capabilities but potentially lose others. The AI you interact with is not incrementally learning from you in real time.

## The Symbiosis Hack
Understanding catastrophic forgetting helps you use AI more realistically. Do not rely on the AI to 'remember' your preferences between sessions. Instead, bring your preferences explicitly every time — via your context document or system prompt. You are compensating for a genuine architectural limitation with your own memory management.

## The Future Proof Superpower
Continual learning — the ability to learn incrementally without catastrophic forgetting — is one of the most active research areas in AI. Solutions being explored include memory-augmented networks, modular learning, and elastic weight consolidation. The AI that can learn like humans do would be transformative. Understanding why current AI cannot do this puts you ahead in evaluating future breakthroughs.

## The Prompt Experiment
Ask AI: 'Explain catastrophic forgetting in neural networks using the analogy of a very forgetful student who, every time they learn a new subject, somehow erases everything they knew before. What architectural solutions have researchers proposed to solve this? Which are most promising?' Evaluate the technical depth of its response.
