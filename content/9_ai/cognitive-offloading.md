## The Black Box Revealed
Cognitive offloading is when you move a thinking task from your brain to an external tool — a calculator, GPS, notebook, or AI. Healthy offloading frees your mental bandwidth for higher-level thinking. Dangerous offloading is when you offload skills you still need to build internally. A student who uses AI to write every essay never develops their writing voice. A student who uses AI for every math step never builds numerical intuition. The skill atrophies because the brain only builds what it uses.

## The Lazy Human Trap
Every shortcut AI offers has a hidden cost. Getting AI to read and summarize every article means you never build deep reading skills. Getting AI to plan your schedule means you never build self-discipline and time-awareness. The cost is paid slowly, in the form of reduced capability, reduced creativity, and reduced problem-solving under pressure.

## The Symbiosis Hack
Apply the 70/30 rule: for any skill you are still developing, do 70% of the work yourself first, then use AI for the remaining 30% (editing, gap-filling, formatting). Once a skill is mastered, you can offload more freely. The question to ask before using AI: 'Am I offloading a skill I have already built, or one I still need to build?'

## The Future Proof Superpower
The students who use AI to augment skills they already have will be dramatically more productive. The ones who use AI to replace skills they never developed will be helpless when AI is unavailable, unreliable, or wrong. Your internal capability is your floor when AI fails.

## The Prompt Experiment
For one week, write your first draft of everything yourself — essays, plans, ideas — before opening an AI. At the end of the week, compare your independent work quality to your AI-assisted work. This gap shows you where you are actually strong and where you are dependent.
