## The Black Box Revealed
AI is not equally accessible. The most powerful AI tools require: reliable internet, a decent device, often a paid subscription, proficiency in English, and some technical literacy. In India, where hundreds of millions of people lack consistent internet access, where dozens of languages are more commonly used than English, and where smartphone quality varies enormously — the distribution of AI benefits is deeply unequal. The students with access to and fluency in AI tools have a learning advantage that compounds every year.

## The Lazy Human Trap
Students who have access to AI assume it is broadly accessible. This creates blind spots in how they think about AI's social impact, and how they design products and services. Building AI products for 'everyone' while designing for an English-speaking, well-connected, relatively wealthy user is a systemic design failure.

## The Symbiosis Hack
If you have AI access, use it maximally — not just for your own benefit, but as practice for eventually helping close the gap. The best-case future is Indian students learning with AI and then building AI tools that work in Telugu, Tamil, Marathi, and the hundreds of other languages and contexts that global AI companies ignore. This is an enormous opportunity.

## The Future Proof Superpower
Bridging the digital divide is not just a social good — it is an economic opportunity. The billion people who do not currently have quality AI access represent an enormous addressable market. The entrepreneurs who build AI tools that work for this population (regional language, low bandwidth, affordable, contextually appropriate) will create enormous value.

## The Prompt Experiment
Ask AI: 'Design an AI-powered educational tool that would genuinely work for a student in rural India who has intermittent 2G internet access, prefers to communicate in their regional language, and cannot afford a paid subscription. What compromises and adaptations would you make?' Evaluate the quality of its understanding of context.
