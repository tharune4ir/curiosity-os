## The Black Box Revealed
AI output quality is directly proportional to input quality. This principle — called GIGO (Garbage In, Garbage Out) — applies at two levels. At training time: if the training data is low-quality, biased, or wrong, the model is fundamentally broken. At inference time (when you use it): if your prompt is vague, unclear, or contradictory, the output will be too. AI does not correct for your unclear thinking. It reflects and amplifies it.

## The Lazy Human Trap
When AI gives a bad answer, the first human instinct is to blame the AI. But usually the problem is the quality of the question. Asking 'tell me about history' is like walking into a library and saying 'tell me something.' You will get a random book. The question is the problem, not the library.

## The Symbiosis Hack
Before writing any prompt, spend 30 seconds thinking: What exactly do I want? What format? What level of detail? What constraints? What's the purpose? This thinking practice — converting fuzzy needs into precise specifications — is the skill that makes AI enormously more useful. It is also the skill that makes you a better communicator in general.

## The Future Proof Superpower
Data quality is the key bottleneck in AI development. As an AI builder, the ability to collect, clean, and structure high-quality data is more valuable than knowing how to code the model itself. Understanding GIGO at the training level puts you ahead in data science and ML engineering.

## The Prompt Experiment
Write the worst possible prompt for a task you need done — maximum vagueness, contradictions, no context. Save the output. Then spend 3 minutes on a high-quality prompt for the same task. Put them side by side. The gap between outputs is a direct measure of prompt skill.
