## The Black Box Revealed
The most dangerous property of AI is not that it gets things wrong — it is that it gets things wrong with complete confidence and beautiful fluency. AI has no internal 'truth sensor.' It cannot fact-check itself. When it does not know something, instead of saying 'I don't know,' it generates the most probable-sounding answer based on patterns — which is often a fluent, perfectly structured, completely wrong 'fact.' Scientists, book titles, historical dates, statistics — all fabricated with confidence.

## The Lazy Human Trap
Students submit AI-generated essays with fake citations — fictional books by real authors, studies that never happened, statistics made up on the spot. When teachers check these references, students face serious consequences. The AI's fluency is the trap: it sounds so authoritative that people forget to verify.

## The Symbiosis Hack
Develop the 'AI Lie Detector' habit: any fact from AI that you plan to use — a statistic, a historical date, a person's quote, a scientific finding — you independently verify on Wikipedia, the textbook, or a news source before using it. Treat AI facts like WhatsApp forwards: interesting, but verify before sharing.

## The Future Proof Superpower
Critical verification is a skill that becomes more valuable as AI-generated content floods the internet. The ability to distinguish fact from fluent fiction — whether from AI or from human propagandists — is arguably the most important information literacy skill of the next decade.

## The Prompt Experiment
Ask AI: 'Tell me about the latest research published in 2024 on a specific niche topic in biology [choose something obscure].' It will likely cite papers, authors, and journals confidently. Now try to find those papers on Google Scholar. Count how many do not exist.
