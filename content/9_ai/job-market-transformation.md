## The Black Box Revealed
AI is not eliminating jobs uniformly. It is restructuring them. A research analyst's job used to be 80% data collection and 20% synthesis/insight. AI flips this: 20% collection (AI-assisted) and 80% synthesis/insight (human-focused). The job still exists, but the valuable part changes. The pattern repeats across fields: tasks that are routine, information-based, or pattern-matching are being automated; tasks requiring judgment, relationships, creativity, and context are becoming more central.

## The Lazy Human Trap
Students plan careers based on current job descriptions without thinking about which parts of those jobs will be automated and which parts will become more central. A student who chooses a career because the specific tasks sound interesting — without considering whether those tasks are automatable — may find the job they trained for no longer exists as they imagined.

## The Symbiosis Hack
Evaluate any career using the RARE framework: Routine (bad, automatable), Adaptive (better), Relational (good, human-essential), Ethical (excellent, requires judgment). A career heavy in RARE's last three is AI-resilient. Medical diagnosis is becoming more routine (AI does it better); medical relationship, communication, and ethical judgment are becoming more valuable. Optimize for the R-A-R-E breakdown.

## The Future Proof Superpower
The students who thrive will be those who continuously update their skills, watch which human capabilities are most complemented by AI, and are willing to shift their professional focus within their field as automation reshapes the task distribution. Adaptability is a meta-skill more important than any specific technical skill.

## The Prompt Experiment
Ask AI: 'Analyze the career of [your dream job]. Break it into specific tasks. For each task, rate its automatability on a scale of 1-10 over the next 15 years. Then tell me what skills someone in this field should develop today to remain valuable in 2040.' Use this output as input for your own career planning.
