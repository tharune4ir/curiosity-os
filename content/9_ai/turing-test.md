## The Black Box Revealed
In 1950, mathematician Alan Turing proposed a test: if a human conversing with a machine (via text) cannot reliably tell they are not talking to another human, the machine can be said to 'think.' Modern AI has arguably passed this test in limited contexts. But many philosophers argue the Turing Test was always the wrong question. Passing it proves the AI can simulate human conversation, not that it is conscious, understands language, or thinks in any meaningful sense.

## The Lazy Human Trap
People equate passing the Turing Test with 'being intelligent' or even 'being conscious.' This is the confusion between performance and understanding — the exact issue the Chinese Room highlights. A very good actor can perform grief without feeling it. A very good AI can perform intelligence without having it.

## The Symbiosis Hack
Design your own personal Turing tests for specific AI capabilities. Not 'can it fool me into thinking it is human?' but 'can it genuinely reason about its own errors?' 'Can it model my specific knowledge gaps?' 'Can it notice when I am confused before I tell it?' These specific performance tests are more useful than the original.

## The Future Proof Superpower
The Turing Test has shaped 70 years of AI research. The next benchmark — what comes after passing the Turing Test — is being debated right now. Proposals include tests for common-sense reasoning, physical world understanding, and genuine causal reasoning. Knowing the history helps you evaluate the next benchmark when it is proposed.

## The Prompt Experiment
Design and run your own mini-Turing Test. Ask AI questions that you think a human could answer from lived experience but AI cannot. Keep testing until you find the boundary. Document what categories of questions consistently reveal the AI-ness. This is original philosophical research you can do for free.
