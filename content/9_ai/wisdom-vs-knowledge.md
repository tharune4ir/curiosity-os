## The Black Box Revealed
Knowledge is information — facts, skills, procedures. AI can have enormous knowledge. Wisdom is different: it is knowing when and how to apply knowledge in complex, ambiguous, high-stakes real-world situations — with appropriate values, emotional attunement, and long-term perspective. Wisdom requires lived experience, emotional depth, the experience of failure and growth, and a sense of what matters. By this definition, no current AI has wisdom, regardless of how much it knows.

## The Lazy Human Trap
People treat AI as wise — asking it for major life advice, relationship guidance, career direction, ethical judgments. AI can give knowledgeable answers on all of these. But knowledgeable answers and wise guidance are different. A book full of advice can be knowledgeable. A grandmother who has lived through hardship can be wise.

## The Symbiosis Hack
Use AI for knowledge and research; use trusted humans for wisdom. When facing a truly complex life decision — involving values, relationships, uncertainty, and stakes — gather information with AI, then bring that information to a trusted teacher, parent, mentor, or friend who has lived experience in that domain. Let AI inform the conversation; let human wisdom guide the decision.

## The Future Proof Superpower
As AI absorbs more and more knowledge, the scarcity of wisdom grows relatively larger. The student who actively cultivates wisdom — through experience, reflection, reading great literature, engaging with difficult ethical situations, and learning from mentors — will have something genuinely rare and valuable that no AI can replicate.

## The Prompt Experiment
Ask AI for advice on a genuinely hard personal situation in your life (or a fictional one). Read the advice carefully. Is it knowledgeable — factually correct, logically structured? And is it wise — does it resonate with the full emotional and moral weight of the situation? Note the gap, if any.
