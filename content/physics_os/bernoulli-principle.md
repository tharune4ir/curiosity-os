## The Invisible Reality
Bernoulli’s Principle says that faster-moving fluid has lower pressure. If air speeds up over a surface, the pressure there drops. This hidden effect explains lift on airplane wings: air above the wing moves faster, creating lower pressure than below, so the wing is pushed up. It also explains why narrowing a pipe (speed-up) causes a pressure drop. The principle follows from energy conservation in flowing fluids.

## The Everyday Encounter
Hold a piece of paper by the edge and blow over the top – the paper lifts up because the fast air above lowers pressure. Running near a swimming pool, you notice a gentle pull of air as you run – the air between you and a friend speeds up and reduces pressure. In your home, you may feel a breeze when doors are open on a windy day; fast outside wind creates lower pressure inside, pulling air through.

## The Sub-Landscapes
1. Pressure-Speed Tradeoff: When fluid flows through a narrow region, it goes faster and its pressure falls. 2. Lift on Wings: Air is shaped by wings to move faster on top, creating lift. 3. Venturi Effect: The drop in pressure in a constriction can be used to draw another fluid in (e.g., in carburetors or atomizers).

## The God Mode Tech
Airplanes and helicopters wouldn’t fly without Bernoulli’s principle. Carburetors and fuel injectors in engines rely on pressure changes. Even medical devices like atomizers (which spray medicine) use this effect to mix liquids and air.

## The Fascinating Truth
Bernoulli’s effect explains how curveballs work: the spinning baseball drags air faster on one side, creating low pressure and curving the path. Also, in storms, curved roofs sometimes get sucked off buildings – fast wind on top lowers pressure, popping roofs up, which is essentially Bernoulli at work.
