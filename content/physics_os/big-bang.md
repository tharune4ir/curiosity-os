## The Invisible Reality
The Big Bang theory describes the universe expanding from a super-hot, super-dense initial state about 13.8 billion years ago【109†L373-L382】. Space itself stretched, carrying galaxies with it. At first, the universe was a hot mix of particles and radiation; as it expanded, it cooled, allowing atoms to form and light to travel freely (we see this as the cosmic microwave background). All current matter and energy come from that expansion and cooling of the early universe.

## The Everyday Encounter
We don’t see the Big Bang directly, but its relics are here. For example, the cosmic microwave background is faint microwave light from the Big Bang’s ‘afterglow’ that all modern electronics (like your microwave oven detector) could pick up. Timekeeping relies on physical constants from the early universe. Even the fact that elements like hydrogen (in water) were formed in the Big Bang’s first minutes connects our tap water to that ancient event.

## The Sub-Landscapes
1. Cosmic Inflation: A brief period right after the start where the universe expanded extremely rapidly. 2. Expansion: Galaxies are moving away from each other; space between them grows. 3. Remnant Radiation: The Big Bang left a nearly uniform glow of microwave radiation across the sky (the CMB).

## The God Mode Tech
Understanding the Big Bang guides the design of telescopes (like Planck) that measure cosmic background. It also sets conditions for particle physics experiments, as accelerators recreate high-energy states similar to the early universe.

## The Fascinating Truth
In the Big Bang, all distances were once almost zero – the entire universe was infinitesimal. The Big Bang wasn’t an explosion in space; it was an expansion of space itself everywhere. Every galaxy is moving away from us not because we are special, but because space is expanding uniformly. Also, because of this, the night sky isn’t ablaze with past explosions (Olbers’ paradox) – the universe had a beginning and is finite in age.
