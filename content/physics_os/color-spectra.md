## The Invisible Reality
White light is made of all colors (wavelengths) combined. When light disperses (like in a prism or raindrop), it splits into a spectrum of colors. Different atoms and molecules absorb and emit specific colors, so each light source has a spectral fingerprint. For example, heated gases emit light at particular colors depending on their elements. Analyzing these spectra reveals composition.

## The Everyday Encounter
A rainbow after rain is the visible spectrum of sunlight refracting through water drops. Digital screens and printers mix red, green, blue to display colors – they work by controlling which colors we see. Flames in a fireplace can have yellow, blue, or green tips depending on substances burning (copper makes green flames, etc.) because those atoms emit specific colors.

## The Sub-Landscapes
1. Dispersion: Different colors travel at different speeds in glass or water, separating when refracted. 2. Emission Lines: Hot elements emit only certain colors (seen in neon signs, fireworks). 3. Absorption Lines: Atoms in stars absorb specific wavelengths; this darkens parts of a star’s spectrum.

## The God Mode Tech
Spectroscopy is used in astronomy to identify star compositions by their spectral lines. Lasers emit very pure single colors (one wavelength). LEDs and lasers in devices are chosen to emit exact colors for efficiency. Soil and water testing can use absorption spectra to detect contaminants.

## The Fascinating Truth
Our eyes see colors because of light’s spectrum. A weird fact: some animals see light beyond our vision (like bees see ultraviolet patterns on flowers invisible to us). Also, every star’s light is filtered by its gas: from afar we can tell if a star has helium or other elements by tiny dark lines in its spectrum.
