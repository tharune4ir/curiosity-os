## The Invisible Reality
Electricity is the flow of electrons or charge through a material. In a wire, electrons drift under an electric field, but the signal (electromagnetic wave) moves at nearly light speed. Even though you can’t see electrons moving, they rearrange instantly. Electricity is also the buildup of charge: static electricity is lots of charge sitting until it jumps (like a spark). It’s the hidden current behind every light bulb and motor.

## The Everyday Encounter
Every appliance in your home shows electricity at work. Turning on a lamp sends electrons through the filament, heating it until it glows. Your phone charging moves electrons from the outlet into the battery. Lightning is a spectacular natural example: huge charge separation in clouds finally discharges as a flash. Even rubbing a balloon on your hair shows it: the balloon sticks to walls due to static charge.

## The Sub-Landscapes
1. Voltage & Current: Voltage (electric pressure) pushes charges; current is the flow rate of charge. 2. Conductors & Insulators: Metals allow electrons to move freely (conductors), rubber and plastic do not (insulators). 3. Circuits: Closed loops where electrons flow; components like resistors convert electrical energy to heat or light.

## The God Mode Tech
All electronics rely on controlled electron flow. Power plants generate current in wires to light cities. Computers use transistors to switch electron flows. Electric motors, generators, and transformers use electricity and magnetism interchangeably (moving charges create magnets and vice versa).

## The Fascinating Truth
Electric signals in wires travel at almost the speed of light, but the electrons themselves move slowly. In fact, the electrons drift only a few millimeters per second, yet the electromagnetic wave moves on the wire almost instantly【75†L129-L132】. Also, magnetism is actually a special case of electricity in motion: Einstein showed that electricity and magnetism are the same force seen from different frames of reference【77†L187-L194】.
