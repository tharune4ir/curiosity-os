## The Invisible Reality
Galaxies are massive collections of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter, all bound by gravity. They come in shapes: spirals (like the Milky Way), ellipticals, irregulars. Most of a galaxy’s mass is unseen dark matter that exerts gravity – it holds the stars in place. Galaxies cluster together into groups and superclusters, forming a cosmic web. The gravity and dark matter together determine how galaxies rotate and evolve.

## The Everyday Encounter
On a clear night you see our galaxy as the Milky Way band across the sky. Andromeda galaxy appears as a faint smudge with the naked eye. Telescope images of distant galaxies look like tiny islands of light. Although we don’t visit them, they are cosmic neighborhoods that even our Sun is part of (Milky Way). Galaxy surveys with telescopes reveal patterns billions of light years away.

## The Sub-Landscapes
1. Spiral vs Elliptical: Spirals have flat rotating disks with spiral arms; ellipticals are more 3D, containing older stars. 2. Rotation Curves: Stars at galaxy edges orbit faster than expected, implying dark matter halos. 3. Galaxy Evolution: Galaxies merge and interact; such collisions can form new stars.

## The God Mode Tech
No direct technology is built from galaxies, but understanding them guides future space telescopes (James Webb, Hubble). Data analysis techniques from surveying galaxies find uses in large data science.

## The Fascinating Truth
Galaxies outnumber what we can imagine – over 100 billion exist in the observable universe. Our Milky Way is on a collision course with Andromeda (in ~4 billion years), but that just forms a larger galaxy.
