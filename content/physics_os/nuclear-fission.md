## The Invisible Reality
Nuclear fission is when a heavy atomic nucleus (like uranium or plutonium) splits into two smaller nuclei plus extra neutrons. This releases a huge amount of energy because the total mass of the pieces is a bit less than the original – that missing mass turns into energy (E=mc²). In practice, each fission event releases energy mostly as kinetic energy of the fragments and neutrons, which we harness as heat.

## The Everyday Encounter
The most familiar use of fission is nuclear power plants. Uranium atoms split in reactor fuel, heating water to produce steam that runs turbines for electricity. Nuclear submarines use fission reactors for propulsion. Fission also powers nuclear bombs, where a rapid chain reaction releases energy in an explosive blast. Smoke detectors often contain a tiny fission source (americium-241) whose emissions help detect smoke particles.

## The Sub-Landscapes
1. Chain Reaction: Fission releases extra neutrons that can cause more fissions, sustaining the reaction. 2. Critical Mass: Sufficient mass of fuel is needed so that each generation of neutrons causes at least one more fission. 3. Products: The split nuclei (fission products) are radioactive, and the released neutrons can be slowed (moderated) in reactors to continue the chain safely.

## The God Mode Tech
Nuclear reactors capture fission energy for power generation without burning fuel, providing large-scale electricity. Neutron radiation from fission is used to make medical isotopes. In naval reactors, submarines achieve long underwater endurance because fission fuel lasts far longer than any chemical fuel.

## The Fascinating Truth
Fission is incredibly energy-dense: just a teaspoon of uranium contains as much energy as burning tons of coal. That’s why splitting a few kilograms in a bomb caused the massive explosions in World War II. Yet in reactors, the same process can run quietly to power cities. It’s the same physics, used in two very different ways.
