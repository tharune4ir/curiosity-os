## The Invisible Reality
Nuclear fusion is when light atomic nuclei combine into a heavier nucleus, releasing energy. In the Sun’s core, hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium, releasing energy that we see as sunlight. Fusion requires extremely high temperatures and pressures so that positively charged nuclei overcome their repulsion (or quantum-tunnel through it). The energy released in fusion is enormous: it’s why stars shine and bombs like the hydrogen bomb are so powerful.

## The Everyday Encounter
On Earth we feel fusion most directly as sunlight, which keeps us warm and powers plants. While we don’t have working fusion reactors yet, the quest for fusion energy (like the ITER project) promises nearly limitless clean power. The heat from thunderstorms is partly due to fusion reactions in cosmic rays creating energetic particles (though small).

## The Sub-Landscapes
1. Conditions: Requires temperatures of millions of degrees to force nuclei close. 2. Fusion Cycle: The proton-proton chain and CNO cycle are how stars fuse hydrogen to helium. 3. Byproducts: Fusion produces neutrons and light nuclei (like helium), and eventually heavier elements in larger stars.

## The God Mode Tech
Experimental fusion reactors (tokamaks, lasers) try to replicate star conditions to generate electricity. Fusion rockets could someday power spacecraft with high efficiency. Magnetic confinement fusion aims to fuse fuel (deuterium, tritium) in doughnut-shaped chambers.

## The Fascinating Truth
Our own Sun is a massive fusion reactor. Every second it fuses 600 million tons of hydrogen, shining with the power of billions of nuclear bombs. A remarkable fact: fusing just 1 gram of hydrogen releases the energy equivalent of burning about 9,000 liters of gasoline.
