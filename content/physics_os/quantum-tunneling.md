## The Invisible Reality
Quantum tunneling lets particles cross energy barriers that they classically couldn’t. If an electron hits a barrier (like a wall), there’s a chance its wavefunction leaks through and appears on the other side. Classically it would bounce back, but quantum mechanically part of its wave can ‘tunnel’ through. This happens even if the particle’s energy is lower than the barrier height, because waves don’t stop abruptly at edges.

## The Everyday Encounter
Tunneling powers many modern devices. In a flash memory (like in USB drives), electrons tunnel through a thin layer to store bits. In the Sun, protons in hydrogen nuclei tunnel through their electrical repulsion barrier to fuse together (fueling sunlight). Tunneling also allows radioactive alpha particles to escape the nucleus in nuclear decay.

## The Sub-Landscapes
1. Barrier Penetration: Finite probability to appear on the other side of a potential barrier. 2. Dependence on Barrier: Thicker/taller barriers make tunneling less likely. 3. Quantum Decay: Tunneling underpins processes like radioactive decay and nuclear fusion.

## The God Mode Tech
Tunnel diodes and semiconductors exploit tunneling for fast switches. Scanning tunneling microscopes (STM) detect surfaces at the atomic level by measuring tunneling currents. Nuclear fusion reactors rely on proton tunneling for fusion at lower temperatures than classically needed.

## The Fascinating Truth
Without tunneling, stars like the Sun couldn’t fuse hydrogen (the thermal energy isn’t enough to overcome repulsion). It’s like a ghost passing through walls: an electron in a stable atom can drop to a lower orbit and release a photon – but it can also tunnel out if given the chance. Tunneling is surprisingly common on tiny scales.
