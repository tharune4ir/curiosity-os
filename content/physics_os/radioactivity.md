## The Invisible Reality
Radioactivity is when an unstable atomic nucleus spontaneously emits particles to become more stable. Common decays are alpha (helium nucleus ejection), beta (electron or positron ejection), and gamma (high-energy photon). Each decay changes the original atom’s identity. This is fundamentally random for each atom, but predictable in large numbers by its half-life (the time in which half the sample decays).

## The Everyday Encounter
Smoke detectors use a bit of americium that emits alpha particles to detect smoke. Bananas are slightly radioactive from naturally occurring potassium-40. Carbon-14 dating in archaeology relies on the known decay rate (half-life) of carbon-14. Radon gas in basements (from uranium in soil) shows radioactivity – it can accumulate to dangerous levels over time.

## The Sub-Landscapes
1. Alpha Decay: Heavy nuclei emit a helium nucleus (2 protons+2 neutrons), decreasing atomic number by 2. 2. Beta Decay: A neutron turns into a proton (beta-minus, electron emitted) or vice versa (beta-plus, positron emitted), changing the element. 3. Gamma Decay: An excited nucleus emits a gamma photon without changing itself (shifting energy states).

## The God Mode Tech
Radioactive isotopes are used in medicine: technetium-99m is a gamma emitter used in diagnostic scans; cobalt-60 gamma rays kill cancer cells. Nuclear power reactors must remove decay heat even after shut-off because radioactivity continues. Smoke detectors and certain luminescent paints use small amounts of radioisotopes.

## The Fascinating Truth
Radioactive decay is truly random – you can’t predict exactly when one atom will decay. However, after many decays, patterns emerge. Astonishingly, tiny amounts of radiation pass through us constantly from natural sources, but our bodies are largely unharmed by these low levels. Even bananas have small radioactivity, but you’d need to eat millions to have any effect!
