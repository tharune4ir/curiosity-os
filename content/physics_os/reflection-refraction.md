## The Invisible Reality
Light travels in straight lines until it hits a surface. Reflection occurs when light bounces off a surface (like a mirror) with the angle in equals angle out. Refraction happens when light passes into a new material (like air to water) and bends, because it changes speed. Each color of light bends slightly differently (blue bends more than red), which is why prisms and rainbows split white light into all colors.

## The Everyday Encounter
Looking into a mirror shows reflection: the angle your eyes view the mirror equals the angle the light left your face. A straw in a glass of water looks bent due to refraction of light entering water. Eyeglasses correct vision by refracting light to focus it on your retina. Even the shimmering heat haze above a hot road is light refracting through layers of hot and cold air.

## The Sub-Landscapes
1. Law of Reflection: Angle of incidence equals angle of reflection (like billiard balls bouncing on a cushion). 2. Snell’s Law: Describes how light bends between materials (faster to slower medium). 3. Lenses and Mirrors: Curved surfaces use reflection or refraction to focus or spread light (cameras, telescopes).

## The God Mode Tech
All imaging devices rely on this: cameras, telescopes, microscopes use lenses and mirrors. Fiber optic cables rely on total internal reflection (a special case of refraction) to trap light. Corrective eyeglasses and contact lenses bend light so we can see clearly.

## The Fascinating Truth
Rainbows and CD rainbows are from refraction: sunlight splitting by water drops or thin films. Also, our eyes see color because different wavelengths interact with materials differently. A neat trick: a pencil in water looks disjointed because the light bends at the water’s surface (refraction).
