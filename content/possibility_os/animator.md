## The Invisible Reality
Animators make pictures move by creating a series of frames. They may draw each frame or move digital models incrementally. For 3D animation, it's like puppeteering: the character has a virtual skeleton, and by changing each bone a little, it moves on screen. In 2D, they draw each important frame and maybe use software to smooth transitions. It's similar to flipbook animation.

## The Mind-Blowing Hook
Animation brings cartoons and CGI movies to life. For example, every second of a movie might have 24 or more frames drawn or computed. Top animators have worked on characters like Shrek or Dory, making them act and emote. Animation today even happens in video games and on smartphones with apps.

## The Player's Life
You start by sketching poses or planning key movements. In 3D, you pose the digital model, set keyframes, and let software fill the in-between frames (tweening). In 2D, you draw each important frame and maybe use software to smooth transitions. You review your work over and over to ensure smooth, natural motion.

## The Skill Tree
Drawing or posing skills (to visualize movement), understanding timing and spacing, and patience (refining frames).
